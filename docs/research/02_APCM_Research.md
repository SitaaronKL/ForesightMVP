**Advanced Primary Care Management**

*The 2025 regulatory unlock that makes 500-patient nurse panels
structurally possible*

Prepared for: Foresight Health interview (Tienlan Sun) • May 2026 •
Dhruv Lalwani

**The short version**

Advanced Primary Care Management is the successor program to Chronic
Care Management, finalized in the calendar year 2025 Medicare Physician
Fee Schedule and live since January 1, 2025. It replaces minute-based
billing with a service-element attestation across three risk-tiered
Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System codes (G0556, G0557, G0558).
Removing the time clock removes the operational ceiling that capped
Chronic Care Management nurse panels around three hundred. It is the
regulatory shape that an artificial intelligence-augmented care model
needs to function at scale, which is why Foresight is building toward
it.

**1. Origin and Timeline**

the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services finalized Advanced
Primary Care Management in the calendar year 2025 Medicare Physician Fee
Schedule Final Rule (CMS-1807-F), published November 2024 and effective
January 1, 2025. The agency's stated motivation was to advance
value-based and accountable care goals by recognizing the longitudinal,
panel-level work that primary care already performs, and by reducing the
administrative drag that has limited adoption of Chronic Care
Management, Principal Care Management (Principal Care Management), and
Transitional Care Management (Transitional Care Management).

Two contextual notes that matter for interview conversations. First, the
Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services explicitly tied Advanced
Primary Care Management to value-based infrastructure: a clinician
already participating in the Medicare Shared Savings Program accountable
care organization, an Innovation Center accountable care organization
(the Realizing Equity, Access, and Community Health model), Making Care
Primary, or Primary Care First automatically satisfies Advanced Primary
Care Management's practice-level requirements for population management
and performance measurement. Advanced Primary Care Management is, in
effect, a bridge fee-schedule code for practices moving into total cost
of care arrangements. Second, the Making Care Primary (Making Care
Primary) demonstration that launched July 1, 2024 was terminated early
on June 30, 2025, announced March 12, 2025. Advanced Primary Care
Management became the durable, fee-schedule-anchored vehicle for many of
the capabilities Making Care Primary was piloting --- ensuring those
care delivery elements remain reimbursable even as the Center for
Medicare and Medicaid Innovation restructures.

Advanced Primary Care Management does not formally replace Chronic Care
Management, Principal Care Management, or Transitional Care Management
in the code set, but it is mutually exclusive with them in any given
month for a given beneficiary. Functionally, for primary care practices
managing a longitudinal panel, Advanced Primary Care Management is the
successor.

**2. The Three Codes (The Core Mechanic)**

  **Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System**   **Level**   **Population**                                                **2025 rate**   **2026 est.**   **Time req.**
  G0556                                           Level 1     0--1 chronic conditions                                       \$15.20         \~\$16.70       NONE
  G0557                                           Level 2     2+ chronic conditions                                         \$48.84         \~\$54          NONE
  G0558                                           Level 3     2+ chronic + Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (dual-eligible)   \$107.07        \~\$117         NONE

These are the published calendar year 2025 national average non-facility
allowables. The calendar year 2026 Final Rule (CMS-1832-F, published
November 5, 2025) raised the care-management code family by
approximately ten percent across the board; the 2026 figures above apply
that scaler. Cents-accurate 2026 numbers should be verified against the
Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services Physician Fee Schedule
Look-up Tool before being quoted in a public deck.

**The killer features**

-   **No time-based threshold.** the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid
    Services explicitly removed the per-month minute requirement that
    constrains Chronic Care Management (20 min) and Principal Care
    Management (30 min). Practitioners attest to capability and delivery
    of service elements, not stopwatch documentation. This is the single
    most important regulatory fact for the Foresight thesis.

-   **Once per calendar month per beneficiary,** billable only by the
    single designated practitioner. Attribution is enforced
    operationally, not by claim system. One "most responsible" primary
    care provider holds the bill.

-   **Patient cost-sharing.** Standard Part B 20% coinsurance applies
    after deductible for G0556 and G0557. Qualified Medicare Beneficiary
    patients (G0558) are fully exempt from cost-sharing and cannot be
    balance-billed even if Medicaid does not reimburse the difference.
    the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services has considered in the
    calendar year 2026 Proposed Rule whether to designate Advanced
    Primary Care Management as a preventive service to eliminate
    cost-sharing entirely; this remains unresolved.

**3. The Service Elements (What Practices Must Be Able to Deliver)**

the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services requires the billing
practice to have the capability to deliver all of the following
elements. Crucially, not every element must be delivered every month ---
the practice must be able to deliver them on demand. The canonical list,
aggregated from the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services Advanced
Primary Care Management page, the American Academy of Family Physicians
coding guide, the National Association of Community Health Centers tip
sheet, and the Federal Register Final Rule text:

-   Patient consent (verbal or written, documented once --- not
    monthly).

-   Initiating visit for new patients or those not seen in 3 years
    (billed separately).

-   24/7 access to care with real-time access to the patient's medical
    record for urgent needs.

-   Continuity of care with a designated care team member with whom the
    patient has a successive routine appointment.

-   Comprehensive care management (systematic needs assessment,
    preventive services, medication reconciliation, self-management
    support).

-   Patient-centered comprehensive care plan --- electronic, shareable
    with patient and caregiver, regularly updated.

-   Management of care transitions: emergency department, hospital,
    skilled nursing facility discharge follow-up; communication of
    clinical information within 7 business days.

-   Practitioner, home, and community-based care coordination, with
    documentation of coordination with home and community service
    providers.

-   Enhanced communication: asynchronous non-face-to-face channels ---
    patient portal, secure messaging, email, phone.

-   Patient population-level management: analyze data to identify gaps
    and risk-stratify the panel.

-   Performance measurement: the Merit-based Incentive Payment System
    reporting under the Value in Primary Care Merit-based Incentive
    Payment System Value Pathway, equivalent electronic clinical quality
    measures, or accountable care organization participation.

Some secondary sources expand this to thirteen elements by splitting
risk stratification, alternative care delivery, and population data
analysis into separate items. For interview rigor, the American Academy
of Family Physicians and the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services
framing of roughly ten or eleven capabilities is most defensible.

**4. Eligibility and Concurrent Billing**

**Patient eligibility**

The patient must have an established primary care relationship with the
billing practitioner --- the "continuing focal point for all needed
health care services." Consent is required once, not monthly.
Attribution is single-practitioner per beneficiary per month. Level 3
(G0558) requires verification of Qualified Medicare Beneficiary status.

**Cannot be billed in the same month for the same patient**

-   Chronic Care Management (99490, 99439, 99487, 99489)

-   Principal Care Management (Principal Care Management, 99424--99427)

-   Transitional Care Management (99495, 99496)

-   Interprofessional consultation codes

-   Virtual check-ins, e-visits, remote evaluation of pre-recorded
    patient information

-   Online digital evaluation and management

**Can be billed concurrently**

-   Remote Physiologic Monitoring (Remote Physiologic Monitoring) ---
    stacks revenue to roughly \$170--\$260+ per member per month for
    high-complexity patients.

-   Remote Therapeutic Monitoring (Remote Therapeutic Monitoring).

-   behavioral health integration / Collaborative Care Model under the
    new 2026 Advanced Primary Care Management-specific add-on codes
    G0568, G0569, G0570.

-   Annual Wellness Visit and standard evaluation and management visits.

**5. Why Advanced Primary Care Management Enables the 500+ Panel**

This is the core operational thesis for the Foresight interview. The
reason a Chronic Care Management nurse caps at three hundred is not
capability --- it is the structure of the code. The twenty-minute
contemporaneous documentation requirement forces three things at once:
an irreducible per-patient time floor, an audit trail that is best
generated synchronously, and a payment cliff if the threshold is missed.
Advanced Primary Care Management dissolves all three:

-   No time minimum means staff effort flows where impact is highest. A
    stable Level 1 patient can be managed via automated portal nudges,
    an annual wellness call, and a quarterly care plan touchpoint. The
    registered nurse's clinical hours go to Level 2 and Level 3 patients
    who need them.

-   Risk-tiered delivery is baked into the code structure. G0556
    patients are well-suited to digital-first and asynchronous tooling.
    G0558 patients warrant hands-on care. The Healthcare Common
    Procedure Coding System layer itself segments the panel for you.

-   Population-level management is an explicit service element, not an
    inference from per-patient minutes. Registries, gap-closure
    dashboards, and outreach campaigns satisfy the requirement at scale
    rather than one beneficiary at a time.

-   Asynchronous and automated communication count toward "enhanced
    communication." A well-designed artificial intelligence agent or
    rules-based outreach engine substitutes for marginal registered
    nurse minutes. text messaging, interactive voice response, portal,
    secure messaging all qualify.

-   General supervision (not direct) remains the standard. Distributed
    and remote care teams are explicitly permitted. The supervising
    practitioner does not need to be physically present; supervision can
    be by phone or telehealth.

The math at five hundred patients on G0557 alone: 500 × \$48.84 =
\$24,420 per month, roughly \$293,000 per year per practice from that
single code. The per-patient per member per month is lower than 99490
stacked with 99439, but the panel-size unlock more than compensates, and
the elimination of minute-tracking overhead drops the variable cost per
touch.

**6. What Advanced Primary Care Management Asks of the Technology
Layer**

If Advanced Primary Care Management is the regulatory shape, the
question becomes: what does an operating system for an Advanced Primary
Care Management-native practice need to do that a Chronic Care
Management platform did not? Seven primitives stand out:

-   Patient-level tier assignment engine. Claims-based or chart-based
    logic that assigns each beneficiary to G0556, G0557, or G0558, with
    monthly re-stratification when a condition list changes or Qualified
    Medicare Beneficiary status flips.

-   Service-element audit trail (not minutes). For each
    beneficiary-month, attestation and log entries proving which of the
    eleven elements were available and which were delivered.
    Documentation artifacts: care plan version, consent record,
    transition follow-up note, portal message logs.

-   Outreach automation layer. text messaging, interactive voice
    response, portal, asynchronous secure messaging --- built to count
    as "enhanced communication" and "comprehensive care management,"
    with content templates that pass the population-management bar.

-   Population analytics and registry dashboard. Gap-closure (Annual
    Wellness Visit, screenings, immunizations, A1c), risk-stratification
    view, panel-level KPIs aligned to the Merit-based Incentive Payment
    System Value in Primary Care Merit-based Incentive Payment System
    Value Pathway or accountable care organization quality measures.

-   Care transitions workflow. admission, discharge, and transfer feed
    integration. emergency department and inpatient discharge worklists
    with seven-day clinical info exchange tracking.

-   Designated-practitioner attribution and consent registry. Single
    source of truth that prevents double-billing, manages re-attribution
    when patients switch primary care providers, and stores the one-time
    consent record.

-   Concurrent billing guardrails. Automated checks preventing Advanced
    Primary Care Management + Chronic Care Management/Principal Care
    Management/Transitional Care Management in the same month while
    permitting Advanced Primary Care Management + Remote Physiologic
    Monitoring/Remote Therapeutic Monitoring/behavioral health
    integration add-ons.

**7. Market State (2025--2026)**

Public the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services adoption data is
not yet published in detail. Secondary commentary from Healtharc,
ChartSpan, Nsight, Prevounce, and others indicates strong interest among
accountable care organizations and primary care groups, with federally
qualified health centers and rural health clinics adopting more slowly
due to workflow re-engineering needs. The vendors actively positioning
around Advanced Primary Care Management enablement include Pearl Health
(whose Signal Action Framework is explicitly Advanced Primary Care
Management-aligned), Aledade (the Medicare Shared Savings
Program-focused, with published policy commentary on the 2025 Physician
Fee Schedule proposed rule), Privia Health, and Agilon Health.
Care-management software incumbents --- ChartSpan, Prevounce,
ThoroughCare, Nsight, HealthSnap, Healtharc, TimeDoc --- are
retrofitting Advanced Primary Care Management workflows onto product
surfaces designed for Chronic Care Management. None is operating
natively as an artificial intelligence-first, provider-not-vendor model.

The 2026 Physician Fee Schedule finalized G0568, G0569, and G0570
(behavioral health integration/Collaborative Care Model add-ons),
allowing total Advanced Primary Care Management-anchored revenue to
reach roughly \$263 per member per month for high-complexity patients
combining Level 3 + Collaborative Care Model + Remote Physiologic
Monitoring. That stack is what economic projections assume when claiming
Advanced Primary Care Management-based care models can be profitable at
panel sizes below the Chronic Care Management-era break-even.

**Voice of the nurse on Advanced Primary Care Management specifically**

There is essentially no public nurse-voice content yet about
G0556/G0557/G0558 by code name. The vendor and billing-firm sources
(Prevounce, Medsien, the American Academy of Family Physicians, the
National Association of Community Health Centers) drive the discourse.
This tells you something useful: most front-line nurses do not yet know
Advanced Primary Care Management by code. They know it as "the new
monthly thing" or as their employer's branded program. If you talk to a
nurse, do not open with code numbers; open with "is your shop billing
the new monthly per-patient code instead of the 20-minute one?" and let
them tell you whether their employer has transitioned.

**8. Foresight Health: The Strategic Fit**

Tienlan's interview prompt --- "Advanced Primary Care Management is
essentially Chronic Care Management without the time-based billing
requirements; how would you architect the system so each nurse can
supervise 500+ patients?" --- maps cleanly onto Foresight's public
positioning. Foresight is an artificial intelligence-native provider,
not a vendor. Their flagship Emi agent is built to handle outreach,
documentation, coordination, and time tracking. The Chronic Care
Management ceiling is a structural constraint of the code; Advanced
Primary Care Management removes that constraint at the regulatory level;
Emi removes the same constraint at the workflow level. The two unlocks
compound.

The clearest argument to walk in with: the bottleneck is not nurse
capacity. It is the twenty-plus minutes of non-clinical overhead bolted
onto every billable hour --- time tracking, contemporaneous
documentation, retries on unreachables, audit prep. Advanced Primary
Care Management removes the minute-tracking. artificial intelligence
agents like Emi remove the documentation and outreach load. Together
they break the three-hundred-patient ceiling, and Foresight's bet is
that being the provider rather than the vendor lets the entire workflow
be redesigned end-to-end, not retrofitted onto a 1990s practice.

**9. Open Questions and Risks Worth Flagging in the Interview**

-   Adoption gating. Advanced Primary Care Management is currently
    limited to practitioners participating in the Medicare Shared
    Savings Program accountable care organization, the Realizing Equity,
    Access, and Community Health model accountable care organization,
    Making Care Primary, or Primary Care First. the Centers for Medicare
    and Medicaid Services has signaled this will expand. Worth
    confirming Foresight's accountable care organization participation.

-   Patient-side cost share. The twenty percent Part B coinsurance on
    G0556 and G0557 creates the same fee-driven attrition risk that
    hampered Chronic Care Management uptake. Mathematica's 2019 study
    showed roughly half of patients with supplemental insurance would
    not have consented to Chronic Care Management out of pocket. The
    calendar year 2026 Proposed Rule discussion of preventive-service
    designation may resolve this; until then, design choices need to
    account for it.

-   Audit shape is different but not absent. The shift from minutes to
    service-element attestation creates a new audit pattern: practices
    will be asked to demonstrate that the eleven elements were actually
    available, not just attested. Documentation infrastructure needs to
    be able to produce the receipts.

-   Concurrency rules. Practices need automated guardrails preventing
    Advanced Primary Care Management and Chronic Care
    Management/Principal Care Management/Transitional Care Management in
    the same month per patient. Operationally, the transition month is
    the trap.

-   Cohort transition strategy. Advanced Primary Care Management and
    Chronic Care Management cannot be billed concurrently for the same
    patient. Practices must move whole cohorts over, which is a
    clinical, billing, and communications project --- not just a code
    switch.
